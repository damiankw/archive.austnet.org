There are some things a person should know and be aware of when spending time on the Internet in general and on AustNet in particular. The tips below are just recommendations which you may choose to ignore, but keep in mind that the reasons behind the suggestions below still apply to you.


Keep the operating system updated and install the security fixes. They haven’t been written for the fun of it but to correct a security problem which people with unfriendly intentions may try to exploit. Make it into a habit to check for security updates on a regular basis. Code Red, Slammer and Blaster attacked known vulnerabilities where fixes had been released long before the viruses hit.


Install a virus scanner and keep it updated. It’s an easy way to protect yourself from less desirable programs running on your computer. You won’t gain more friends by installing it, but at least you won’t lose any either. It’s good to keep real time protection on as some virii try to disable antivirus programs. It’s better to block and delete the virus before it has a chance to do so. You may also want to install a spyware killer to help protect your privacy.


Personally I’d count a firewall as less important than installing the security patches and keeping a freshly updated virus scanner, but even though they may be annoying at times, they protect against what some of the things not covered by the security updates and virus protection and when you’re connected to the Internet, you will be scanned or attacked by programs testing long lists of addresses.


You may have seen this before, but pick a password which isn’t guessable, yet easy for you to remember. A phrase or song line (more than one word) is a good choice as you will remember it. It’s good to include digit and non-alphanumerical characters as it makes the process of trying to guess the password harder. Also keep the password private and don’t share it. If you’re asked for it on AUSTnet by someone who claims to be an AUSTnet official such as an IRC operator or ASD helper, don’t give it out. IRC operators don’t care, and the ASD helper doesn’t need it.


Try not to be paranoid, but at the same time, don’ t think that you’re completely safe from everything unwanted just because you’ve covered the points mentioned above. A malicious program doesn’t have to qualify as a virus, worm or trojan in order to be malicious.


If you dislike spam and get spammed, don’t click on it, don’t go there. Spammers rarely have your best at heart and do it to achieve their own goals whether it’s to make money, get more supporters or to infect your computer. If no one supports these activities, it would cease. If you click on it, you support it and encourage the spammers to go on. Additionally, don’t spam as it’s a sure way to gain a lot of enemies, including ASD members and IRC operators working to rid the network from spam.


Try to learn and understand the basic IRC commands. This makes you less dependent on others to help you, who may not be around at times when you actually do need help. The help files are a good source of knowledge and they are usually meant to be written in a way which can be understood. You shouldn’t need an Enigma code breaker to decipher it. If you get stuck, you can ask for help in #Help or #ASD if its services related.


The rules of the network are there to provide you with a better overall service. Knowing the rules of the playground can save you from the consequences of breaking them. Furthermore, the volunteers who try to make the network run smoothly already have enough to do without you adding to it.


Curiosity is good, but try not to get too excited and adventurous. If you want to find out what some script or command does, check the help files, ask someone who may know or find out or simply look at the code to see if it seems to do something useful. You don’t want to turn your IRC client into a spam bot. If you get a message when you join a channel regarding how to stop spamming, don’t try it out as it’s not likely to do what the message claims. If there were a short and simple way to stop spam with simple commands, it would already be included in the IRC servers.


You’re most likely aware that some people lie from time to time to different degrees. Don’t believe in everything you hear on the Internet just because you hear it. Someone may be telling you the truth, but at times it may be a lie. There is no need to go completely paranoid, but use common sense and think about if it makes sense or not. This doesn’t only apply on the Internet, but IRC is a text medium and the signs you may see in real life when talking to someone don’t exist. Keep a reasonable level of caution and you may save yourself from a lot of pain.


If you misbehave in a channel, you’re likely to get banned so don’t be surprised if it happens. If you’re an op banning someone from a channel, do it when there is a reason and not just for fun. When you ban, try to make the ban message a polite one. No one likes to be insulted or ridiculed. The reaction of senseless bans may be that your channel gets flooded as payback. On the other hand, if you’re the one doing the flooding, you’re not likely to get the ban lifted as the channel operators are unlikely to appreciate the flood and you will end up spending a lot of time and energy to maintain an aggravation which won’t get you anywhere. Just like in real life, the people you encounter on Internet have feelings and emotions. If you treat them badly, they won’t like you and they will treat you accordingly and so will their friends.


Just as in real life, always use common sense. If you see something, think about what it may mean. Usually there is some kind of logic behind things. If you need help from someone, try to think about what he or she may need to know in order to help you. If you send an e- mail, you will save time by sending the needed information at once as the replier doesn’t need to mail back and ask for it.
